Don't Drink That!

& Other Things Your Lola Said
(That Aren't True)

Growing up in a Filipino household means you've probably been told to rub garlic on your forehead, don't drink Coke after eating mangoes, or that rain + a shower = instant death. But how much of Lola's medical wisdom is actually true? Let's bust some of these beloved tsismis-based health myths — with science, of course. 🩺💊


❌MYTH vs TRUTH✅

❌ MYTH #1: Sleeping with wet hair can lead to blindness.

TRUTH: There's no link between wet hair and blindness. However, sleeping with wet hair may lead to fungal scalp infections or hair breakage, but most of the time, it will only give you a wet pillow and messy hair day — not blindness.
❌ MYTH #2: Washing tired hands or taking a bath after exercising or playing outdoor games/sports can cause “pasma.”

TRUTH: “Pasma” is not recognized by modern medicine. Hand tremors, sweaty hands, numbness, and pain in the hands are symptoms that are often related to diabetes mellitus, thyroid dysfunction, and nervous system dysfunction. Pay a quick visit to a health professional if you regularly experience these symptoms.
❌ MYTH #3: You can get warts from frog's urine.

TRUTH: Warts are caused by the Human Papillomavirus (HPV), not frog or toad pee. Their bumpy skin just resembles warts, which might be where this myth came from.
❌ MYTH #4: Not wiping sweat causes pneumonia.

TRUTH: Pneumonia is caused by bacteria, viruses, or fungi — not dried sweat. While it's more hygienic to dry off, not doing so won't give you lung infection.
❌ MYTH #5: Toothpaste helps heal burns.

TRUTH: Toothpaste may feel cool, but it can actually trap heat and worsen burns. It contains irritants like menthol and baking soda, which aren't meant for broken or sensitive skin. Stick to cool water and real burn ointments.


💖 Lola Said It, but...

Lola said Vicks can cure anything, but Vicks said, “Girl, I'm just menthol.”

Lola said rain will make you sick but germs said, “Don't blame the weather, blame us.”

Lola said your first menstrual blood is a natural facial, but science said, “Hon, that's bacteria.”

Lola said swallowed gum stays in your belly forever, but your tummy said, “It's out by tomorrow.”

Lola said stepping over a child stunts their growth, but genes said, “Respectfully, no.”



Why Health Myths Spread 🩺

Health myths often spread because they're simple, memorable, and shared with love — especially in times or places where medical help was far away. These beliefs were passed down like heirlooms, wrapped in care and lived experience. But just because something is repeated doesn't mean it's rooted in science. As future nurses, our mission isn't to embarrass or scold, but to gently guide — explaining the truth with compassion, and offering evidence with empathy. Speak science with softness.


To our Lolas! 💖👵

We grew up hearing, “Huwag maligo pag bagong ligo ang buhok,” or “Ipahid mo 'yan ng Vicks!” — and honestly, we love that. It's part of who we are. Our lolas, titas, and even manangs taught us what they knew with love and pride, drawn from experience and tradition. Their advice often came from a place of care and caution — and that should always be honored.

But as we grow in knowledge, we must learn to ask: Is it true? Is it safe? Is it still the best we can offer? Not everything that's old-school is a gold-standard. And that's okay. So let's listen to our elders with open ears and grateful hearts — and then fact-check with science. Curiosity and respect can exist together. After all, the best kind of healing comes from blending wisdom and evidence, not choosing one over the other.
